# Example — logo-review

## Input

> Review this logo. It's for a Web3 wallet app called "Valt." The mark is a stylized V shape that doubles as a vault door hinge. Wordmark uses a geometric sans-serif. The V mark is thick, symmetrical, and works in both black and a gradient blue-to-purple version. Designed to work on dark and light backgrounds.

## Output

**Concept & Originality — 8/10**
V-as-vault is a clean double read — the name reinforces the mark without being too literal. It's the right level of conceptual subtlety for a wallet product. The risk: "vault" visual language is becoming crowded in crypto (shield, lock, vault combinations are everywhere). The execution of the hinge detail is what differentiates it — lean into that in marketing.

**Shape & Form — 7/10**
Thick and symmetrical is a good call for a mark that needs to work at small sizes (app icon, favicon). Symmetry aids recall. The concern is whether the hinge detail survives at 16px — test this at actual favicon size before finalizing.

**Typography — 6/10**
Geometric sans-serif is the safe choice for Web3 but it needs to do more work. What weight? What spacing? Tight tracking on "VALT" in all-caps could feel premium and intentional. Loose tracking reads casual. Don't let the wordmark be an afterthought to the mark.

**Versatility**
Dark/light background support is essential — good that it's built in. The gradient version looks strong in digital contexts but will fail in single-color use cases (embroidery, stamps, one-color print). Ensure the flat version is equally strong without the gradient doing the heavy lifting.

**Brand Fit — 8/10**
Feels like a credible wallet product. Projects trust and security without being boring. The blue-to-purple gradient is well-worn in crypto but still effective for the category.

---

**One thing to fix:** Test the hinge detail at 16px and 32px. If it disappears, simplify the mark for small sizes or create an icon-specific variant.
